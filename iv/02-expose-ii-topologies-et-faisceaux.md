# Expose II — Topologies and Sheaves

By J.-L. Verdier.

After the definition of topologies and pretopologies (§1) and of sheaves of sets (§2), §3 takes up the central theorem
of this expose: the existence of the sheaf associated to a presheaf (3.4). In order to cover all cases encountered in
practice, the existence of this functor is proved for presheaves on a $U$-site (3.0.2).

Sections 4 and 5 draw the consequences of this theorem for the behavior of inductive and projective limits in categories
of sheaves. In §6, one defines and studies sheaves with values in arbitrary categories, immediately turning attention to
sheaves of rings, groups, modules, and so on.

## 1. Topologies. Covering Families. Pretopologies

**Definition 1.1.** A topology on a category $C$ is the datum, for every object $X$ of $C$, of a set $J(X)$ of sieves of
$X$, subject to the following axioms:

- (T1) Stability under base change. For every object $X$ of $C$, every sieve $R \in J(X)$, and every morphism $f : Y \to
  X$, with $Y \in \operatorname{ob}(C)$, the sieve $R \times_X Y$ of $Y$ belongs to $J(Y)$.
- (T2) Local character. If $R$ and $R'$ are two sieves of $X$, if $R \in J(X)$, and if, for every $Y \in
  \operatorname{ob}(C)$ and every morphism $Y \to R$, the sieve $R' \times_X Y$ of $Y$ belongs to $J(Y)$, then $R'$
  belongs to $J(X)$.

[PDF p. 235]

- (T3) For every object $X$ of $C$, $X$ belongs to $J(X)$.

**1.1.1.** The sieves belonging to $J(X)$ will be called the sieves covering $X$, or also the refinements of $X$. From
axioms (T1), (T2), and (T3), one immediately deduces that the set of sieves covering $X$ is stable under finite
intersections, and that every sieve containing a covering sieve is a covering sieve. The set $J(X)$, ordered by
inclusion, is therefore cofiltered (I 8).

**1.1.2.** Let $C$ be a category, and let $T$ and $T'$ be two topologies on $C$. The topology $T$ is said to be finer
than the topology $T'$ if, for every object $X$ of $C$, every refinement of $X$ for the topology $T'$ is a refinement of
$X$ for the topology $T$. In this way one defines an order structure on the set of topologies.

**1.1.3.** Let $(T_i)$ be a family of topologies on $C$. For every object $X$ of $C$, the sieves of $X$ that are
covering for all the topologies $T_i$ verify axioms (T1), (T2), and (T3), and therefore define a topology: the
intersection topology of the $T_i$, that is, the lower bound of the $T_i$. It is the finest of the topologies that is
less fine than all the topologies $T_i$. Consequently the family $(T_i)$ admits an upper bound: the intersection
topology of the topologies finer than each of the $T_i$.

[PDF p. 236]

**1.1.4.** In particular, the datum $J(X)$ equal to the set of all sieves of $X$ is a topology finer than every topology
on $C$; we shall call it the discrete topology.

There exists a topology less fine than every topology on $C$: the topology for which $J(X)=\{X\}$ for every $X$ of $C$.
This topology is called the coarse or chaotic topology.

**1.1.5.** A category $C$ equipped with a topology is called a site. The category $C$ is called the category underlying
the site.

**Definition 1.2.** Let $C$ be a site and let $X$ be an object of $C$. A family of morphisms $(f_i : X_i \to X)_{i \in
I}$ is said to be covering if the sieve generated by the family $f_i$ (I 4.3.3) is a sieve covering $X$.

**1.1.6.** Let $C$ be a category. Suppose we are given, for each object $X$ of $C$, a set of families of morphisms with
target $X$. Then there exists a topology $T$ that is the least fine of the topologies for which the given families are
covering, namely the intersection (1.1.3) of all the topologies in question. This topology is called the topology
generated by the given sets of families of morphisms. It is difficult, in general, to describe all covering sieves of
this topology. However the situation is more pleasant in the following case.

**Definition 1.3.** Let $C$ be a category. A pretopology on $C$ is the datum, for each object $X$ of $C$, of a set
$\operatorname{Cov}(X)$ of families of morphisms with target $X$, subject to the following axioms:

[PDF p. 237]

- (PT0) For every object $X$ of $C$, the morphisms in the families of morphisms of $\operatorname{Cov}(X)$ are
  squarable. Recall that a morphism $Y \to X$ is said to be squarable if, for every morphism $Z \to X$, the fiber
  product $Z \times_X Y$ is representable.
- (PT1) Stability under base change. For every object $X$ of $C$, every family $(X_i \to X)_{i \in I}$ belonging to
  $\operatorname{Cov}(X)$, and every morphism $Y \to X$, with $Y \in \operatorname{ob}(C)$, the family $(X_i \times_X Y
  \to Y)_{i \in I}$ belongs to $\operatorname{Cov}(Y)$.
- (PT2) Stability under composition. If $(X_i \to X)_{i \in I}$ belongs to $\operatorname{Cov}(X)$, and if for each $i
  \in I$ a family $(X_{ij} \to X_i)_{j \in J_i}$ belongs to $\operatorname{Cov}(X_i)$, then the composite family
  $(X_{ij} \to X)$ belongs to $\operatorname{Cov}(X)$.
- (PT3) The family reduced to the identity morphism $X \to X$ belongs to $\operatorname{Cov}(X)$.

**1.3.1.** Definition 1.1.6 allows us, for a given pretopology on $C$, to consider the topology on $C$ generated by this
pretopology. Note that if $C$ is a category in which fiber products are representable, then every topology $T$ on $C$
can be defined by a pretopology, namely the one for which $\operatorname{Cov}(X)$ is formed by all families covering $X$
for the topology $T$.

**Proposition 1.4.** Let $C$ be a category, $E$ a pretopology on $C$, $T$ the topology defined by the pretopology $E$
(1.1.6), and $X$ an object of $C$. Denote by $J_E(X)$ the set of sieves of $X$ generated by families of the pretopology,
and by $J_T(X)$ the set of refinements of $X$ for

[PDF p. 238]

the topology $T$. Then $J_E(X)$ is cofinal in $J_T(X)$. In other words, for a sieve $R$ of $X$ to belong to $J_T(X)$, it
is necessary and sufficient that there exist a sieve $R' \in J_E(X)$ such that $R' \subset R$.

**Proof.** Let $J'(X)$, for every object $X$ of $C$, be the set of sieves of $X$ that contain a sieve of $J_E(X)$.
Evidently $J'(X) \subset J_T(X)$. To show that $J'(X)=J_T(X)$, it suffices to show that the datum of the $J'(X)$ defines
a topology on $C$. Now the $J'(X)$ evidently verify axioms (T1) and (T3). It remains to verify (T2).

For this it suffices to prove that, if $R'$ is a subsieve of $R \in J_E(X)$ such that for every $Y \to R$ the sieve $R'
\times_X Y$ of $Y$ belongs to $J'(Y)$, then the sieve $R'$ belongs to $J'(X)$. But the sieve $R$ is generated by a
family $(X_i \to X)$ belonging to $\operatorname{Cov}(X)$, and for every $i$ the sieve $R' \times_X X_i$ contains a
sieve generated by a family $(X_{ij} \to X_i)$ belonging to $\operatorname{Cov}(X_i)$. We deduce that the sieve $R'$
contains a sieve generated by the composite family $(X_{ij} \to X)$. Therefore, by axiom (PT2), $R'$ contains a sieve of
$J_E(X)$, and consequently belongs to $J'(X)$. QED.

## 2. Sheaves of Sets

**Definition 2.1.** Let $C$ be a site whose underlying category is a $U$-category. A presheaf $F$ with values in $U$-Ens
is said to be **separated** (respectively is a **sheaf**) if for every sieve $R$ covering $X$, an object of $C$, the map

$$ \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(X,F) \to \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R,F) $$

is injective (respectively bijective). The full subcategory of $\widehat{C}$

[PDF p. 239]

whose objects are the sheaves is called the category of sheaves of sets on $C$, and is most often denoted
$\widetilde{C}$ (1). When no ambiguity results, we shall simply say category of sheaves on $C$; by contrast, we shall
sometimes be more precise and say category of sheaves with values in $U$-Ens, or category of $U$-sheaves.

**Proposition 2.2.** Let $C$ be a $U$-category, and let $\mathcal{F}=(F_i)_{i \in I}$ be a family of $U$-presheaves on
$C$. For each object $X$ of $C$, denote by $J_{\mathcal{F}}(X)$ the set of sieves $R \to X$ such that for every morphism
$Y \to X$ of $C$ with target $X$, the sieve $R \times_X Y$ has the following property: the map

$$ \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(Y,F_i) \to \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R \times_X Y,F_i) $$

is bijective (respectively injective) for every $i \in I$. Then the sets $J_{\mathcal{F}}(X)$ define a topology on $C$,
which is the finest of the topologies for which each $F_i$ is a sheaf (respectively a separated presheaf).

**Proof.** The $J_{\mathcal{F}}(X)$ evidently verify axioms (T1) and (T3). It remains to show that they verify (T2). For
this it suffices to show that:

1. If $R' \to R \to X$ are two sieves of $X$, with $R \in J_{\mathcal{F}}(X)$ and such that for every $Y \to R$ (where
   $Y$ is an object of $C$) the sieve $R' \times_X Y$ belongs to $J_{\mathcal{F}}(Y)$, then the sieve $R'$ belongs to
   $J_{\mathcal{F}}(X)$.

2. If $R' \to R \to X$ are two sieves of $X$ such that $R' \in J_{\mathcal{F}}(X)$, then the sieve $R$ belongs to
   $J_{\mathcal{F}}(X)$.

Note that, in case 2), for every $Y \to R$ (where $Y$ is an object of $C$) the sieve $R' \times_X Y$ belongs to
$J_{\mathcal{F}}(Y)$. Now, in cases 1) and 2), $R$ is the inductive limit of the $Y$, objects of $C$, over $R$ (I 3.4).

> (1) or $\widehat{C}$, depending on the mood of the typewriter.

[PDF p. 240]

Inductive limits in $\widehat{C}$ are universal (I 3.3). Consequently, in cases 1) and 2), $R'$ is the inductive limit
of the $R' \times_X Y$. Now, in cases 1) and 2), $R' \times_X Y$ belongs to $J_{\mathcal{F}}(Y)$. Passing to the
inductive limit over the objects $Y$ of $C$ over $R$, one deduces that the map

$$ \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R,F_i) \to \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R',F_i) $$

is a bijection (respectively an injection) for every $i \in I$. Consequently the maps

$$ \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(X,F_i) \to \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R',F_i) $$

$$ \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(X,F_i) \to \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R,F_i) $$

are, in cases 1) and 2), bijections (respectively injections).

Moreover, hypotheses 1) and 2) are visibly stable under arbitrary base change $Y \to X$ ($Y$ an object of $C$). It
follows that for every object $Y$ of $C$ over $X$ and every $i \in I$, the maps

$$ \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(Y,F_i) \to \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R \times_X Y,F_i) $$

$$ \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(Y,F_i) \to \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R' \times_X Y,F_i) $$

are, in both cases, bijections (respectively injections); this shows that $R'$ and $R$ belong to $J_{\mathcal{F}}(X)$.
QED.

**Corollary 2.3.** Let $C$ be a $U$-category, and for every $X$ of $C$ let $K(X)$ be a set of sieves of $X$. Suppose
that the $K(X)$ verify axiom (T1) of (1.1). For a presheaf $F$ to be a sheaf (respectively a separated presheaf) for the
topology generated (1.1.6) by the $K(X)$, it is necessary and sufficient that for every object $X$ of $C$ and every
sieve $R \in K(X)$, the map

$$ \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(X,F) \to \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R,F) $$

be a bijection (respectively an injection).

[PDF p. 241]

**Corollary 2.4.** In particular, let $C$ be a $U$-category equipped with a pretopology. For a presheaf $F$ to be a
sheaf (respectively a separated presheaf), it is necessary and sufficient that for every object $X$ of $C$ and every
family $(X_{\alpha} \to X)_{\alpha \in A}$ belonging to $\operatorname{Cov}(X)$, the diagram of sets

$$ F(X) \to \prod_{\alpha \in A} F(X_{\alpha}) \rightrightarrows \prod_{(\alpha,\beta)\in A\times A}
F(X_{\alpha}\times_X X_{\beta}) $$

be exact (respectively the map

$$ F(X) \to \prod_{\alpha \in A} F(X_{\alpha}) $$

be injective).

**Proof.** Apply 2.3, then (I 3.5) and (I 2.12).

With Corollary 2.4 one recovers the definition given in [1].

**Definition 2.5.** Let $C$ be a $U$-category. The **canonical topology** of $C$ is the finest topology for which the
representable functors are sheaves (2.2). A sieve covering $X$ for the canonical topology will be called a **universally
strict epimorphic sieve**. A family covering for the canonical topology will be called a **universally strict epimorphic
family**. When, moreover, the morphisms of the covering family are squarable, the family will be said to be
**universally effective epimorphic** [2].

**Remark 2.5.1.** For almost all sites that have had to be used up to now, the topology is less fine than the canonical
topology; in other words, the representable functors on $C$ are sheaves, i.e. the covering families of $C$ are
universally strict epimorphic.

[PDF p. 242]

The only exception to this rule is the site $\widetilde{C}$ studied in no. 5 (whose topology is finer, and most often
strictly finer, than the canonical topology).

**Proposition 2.6.** For a sieve $R \to X$ to be universally strict epimorphic, it is necessary and sufficient that, for
every object $Y \to X$ of $C$ over $X$ and every object $Z$ of $C$, the map

$$ \operatorname{Hom}_C(Y,Z) \to \varprojlim_{C/(Y \times_X R)} \operatorname{Hom}_C(-,Z) $$

be a bijection.

**Proof.** Immediate by applying 2.2 and then (I 5.3).

**Remarks 2.7.**

1. Proposition 2.6 gives a characterization of the universally strict epimorphic sieves of a category $C$, independent
   of the universe in which the presheaves take their values, with the sole condition that the morphism sets
   $\operatorname{Hom}(X,Y)$ of $C$ belong to this universe. It thus allows one to define the canonical topology for
   every category $C$, without needing to specify universes.

2. Let $C$ be a site whose underlying category is a $U$-category, let $F$ be a $U$-presheaf, and let $V$ be a universe
   containing $U$. The category underlying $C$ is a $V$-category, and $F$ may be considered as a $V$-presheaf. The
   $U$-presheaf $F$ is a sheaf (respectively a separated presheaf) if and only if the $V$-presheaf $F$ is a sheaf
   (respectively a separated presheaf). In other words, the condition that $F$ be a sheaf (respectively a separated
   presheaf) does not depend (in the sense just

[PDF p. 243]

   specified) on the universe in which the presheaf $F$ takes its values. In particular, let $C$ be a site and $F$ a
   $U$-presheaf; one says that $F$ is a sheaf (respectively a separated presheaf) if there exists a universe $V$
   containing $U$ such that the category $C$ is a $V$-category and such that $F$ is a $V$-sheaf (respectively a separated
   $V$-presheaf). This property does not depend on the universe $V$.

## 3. Sheaf Associated to a Presheaf

**Definition 3.0.1.** Let $C$ be a site. A **topologically generating family** (or, when no confusion results,
**generating family**) of $C$ is a set $G$ of objects of $C$ such that every object of $C$ is the target of a covering
family of morphisms of $C$ (1.2) whose sources are elements of $G$.

**Definition 3.0.2.** Let $U$ be a universe. A **$U$-site** is a site $C$ whose underlying category is a $U$-category (I
1.1), which possesses a small topologically generating family. Let $C$ be a $U$-category; a **$U$-topology** on $C$ is a
topology on $C$ making $C$ into a $U$-site. A site $C$ is said to be **$U$-small**, or by abuse of language **small**,
if the category underlying $C$ is small (I 1.0).

**3.0.3.** It follows immediately from the definitions that every topology finer than a $U$-topology is a $U$-topology,
and that a small site is a $U$-site.

[PDF p. 244]

**Proposition 3.0.4.** Let $C$ be a $U$-site, and let $G$ be a small topologically generating family of $C$. For every
$X \in \operatorname{ob} C$, denote by $J_G(X)$ the set of sieves covering $X$ generated by a family of morphisms

$$ (Y_{\alpha} \to X),\quad \alpha \in A,\quad \text{where } Y_{\alpha}\in G. $$

1. The set $J_G(X)$ is small.
2. The set $J_G(X)$ is cofinal in the set $J(X)$ of all sieves covering $X$, ordered by inclusion.
3. For every $R \in J_G(X)$, there exists a small epimorphic family (I 10.2)

$$ (u_{\alpha} : Y_{\alpha} \to R),\quad \alpha \in A,\quad \text{with } Y_{\alpha}\in G. $$

**Proof.** 1) Put $A(X)=\coprod_{Y \in G}\operatorname{Hom}(Y,X)$. The set $A(X)$ is small (I 0) and
$\operatorname{card}(J_G(X)) \leq 2^{\operatorname{card}(A(X))}$. Consequently $J_G(X)$ is small.

2) Let $R \in J(X)$. Put $A(R)=\coprod_{Y \in G}\operatorname{Hom}(Y,R)$ and let $R'$ be the sieve of $X$ generated by
the family $(Y \to R \to X)$, $u \in A(R)$. We have $R' \subset R$, and it suffices to show that $R'$ is covering. By
axiom (T2) of 1.1, it suffices to show that for every morphism $Z \to R$, $Z \in \operatorname{ob} C$, the sieve
$R' \times_X Z$ of $Z$ is covering. But the sieve $R' \times_X Z$ contains the sieve generated by the family of all
morphisms $Y \to Z$ with $Y \in G$, a family which is covering by hypothesis. The sieve $R' \times_X Z$ of $Z$ is
therefore covering (axiom T2 of 1.1).

3) Let $R \in J_G(X)$. The family $(Y_u \to R)$, $u \in A(R)=\coprod_{Y \in G}\operatorname{Hom}(Y,R)$, is epimorphic by
hypothesis. Now, for every $Y \in \operatorname{ob} C$, $\operatorname{Hom}(Y,R)$ is contained in $\operatorname{Hom}(Y,X)$,
which is small. Consequently $A(R)$ is small.

**3.0.5.** Let $C$ be a $U$-site, let $V$ be a universe such that $C \in V$ and $U \subset V$, and let $G$ be a
$U$-small topologically generating family of $C$. The category $\widehat{C}$ of presheaves of $U$-sets on $C$ is a
$V$-category (I 1.1.1). Let

[PDF p. 245]

$X$ be an object of $C$. The set $J(X)$ of sieves covering $X$ is $V$-small and, ordered by inclusion, is cofiltered
(1.1.1). For every $U$-presheaf $F$, the inductive limit

$$ \varinjlim_{J(X)} \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(-,F) $$

is therefore representable by an element of $V$ (I 2.4.1). Moreover, it follows from (3.0.4 3)) that, for every $R \in
J_G(X)$, $\operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R,F)$ is $U$-small; and since $J_G(X)$ is a $U$-small set cofinal in $J(X)$
(loc. cit.), it follows from (I 2.4.2) that $\varinjlim_{J(X)} \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(-,F)$ is $U$-small.
Choose then, for every $F$ and every $X$, an element of $U$ representing this inductive limit and put

$$ LF(X)=\varinjlim_{J(X)} \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(-,F). $$

Let $g : Y \to X$ be a morphism of $C$. The base-change functor $g^* : J(X) \to J(Y)$ defines a map

$$ LF(g) : LF(X) \to LF(Y) $$

which makes $X \mapsto LF(X)$ into a presheaf on $C$.

Since the morphism $\operatorname{id}_X : X \to X$ is an element of $J(X)$, for every object $X$ of $C$ we have a map

$$ \ell(F)(X) : F(X) \to LF(X), $$

which visibly defines a morphism of functors

$$ \ell(F) : F \to LF. $$

It is moreover clear that $F \mapsto LF$ is a functor in $F$ and that the morphisms $\ell(F)$ define a morphism

$$ \ell : \operatorname{Id} \to L. $$

Finally let $R \to X$ be a refinement of $X$. The definition of $LF(X)$ and (I 1.4) provide a map

$$ Z_R : \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R,F) \to \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(X,LF), $$

[PDF p. 246]

and for every morphism of $C$, $g : Y \to X$, the definition of the functor $LF$ shows that the diagram below is
commutative:

```text
Z_R:       Hom_{C^}(R, F)          -> Hom_{C^}(X, LF)
              |                         |
              v                         v
Z_{R x_X Y}: Hom_{C^}(R x_X Y, F)  -> Hom_{C^}(Y, LF)
```

where the vertical arrows are the evident arrows. We now copy [2].

**Lemma 3.1.**

1. For every refinement $i_R : R \to X$ and every $u : R \to F$, the diagram

    ```text
    F --ell(F)--> LF
    ^             ^
    | u           | Z_R(u)
    R --i_R---->  X
    ```

    is commutative.

2. For every morphism $v : X \to LF$, there exists a refinement $R$ of $X$ and a morphism $u : R \to F$ such that
   $Z_R(u)=v$.

3. Let $Y$ be an object of $C$ and let $u,v : Y \to F$ be two morphisms such that $\ell(F)\circ u=\ell(F)\circ v$. Then
   the kernel of the pair $(u,v)$ is a refinement of $Y$.

4. Let $R$ and $R'$ be two refinements of $X$, and let $u : R \to F$ and $u' : R' \to F$ be two morphisms. For
   $Z_R(u)=Z_{R'}(u')$, it is necessary and sufficient that $u$ and $u'$ coincide on a refinement $R'' \to R \times_X
   R'$.

**Proof.** The only nontrivial assertion is assertion 1). We must show that $Z_R(u)\circ i_R=\ell(F)\circ u$. For this
it suffices to show (I 3.4) that the composites of these morphisms with every morphism $g : Y \to R$ ($Y$ an object

[PDF p. 247]

of $C$) are equal. Now consider $f=i_R\circ g$ and the fiber product $R \times_X Y$:

```text
F  --ell(F)--> LF
^              ^
| u            | Z_R(u)
R  --i_R---->  X
^              ^
| g'           | f
R x_X Y --i'-> Y
```

In the above diagram, $i'$ is a monomorphism that admits a section. Consequently (I 3.3) $i'$ is an isomorphism. Now by
definition of the morphism $\ell(F)$, the morphism $Z_{R \times_X Y}(u\circ g')$ is equal to $\ell(F)\circ u\circ g'$.
On the other hand, the commutativity of diagram (*) gives the equality $Z_{R \times_X Y}(u\circ g')=Z_R(u)\circ f$, and
consequently we indeed have the equality $\ell(F)\circ u\circ g=Z_R(u)\circ i_R\circ g$.

**Proposition 3.2.**

1. The functor $L$ is left exact (I 2.3.2).
2. For every presheaf $F$, $LF$ is a separated presheaf.
3. The presheaf $F$ is separated if and only if the morphism $\ell(F) : F \to LF$ is a monomorphism. The presheaf $LF$
   is then a sheaf.
4. The following properties are equivalent:

i) $\ell(F) : F \to LF$ is an isomorphism.

ii) $F$ is a sheaf.

**Proof.** 1) It suffices to show (I 3.1) that, for every object $X$ of $C$, the functor $F \mapsto LF(X)$ commutes with
finite projective limits. But, by definition of

[PDF p. 248]

the projective limit, the functor

$$ F \mapsto \operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat{C}}(R,F),\qquad R \to X \in \operatorname{ob}(J(X)), $$

commutes with projective limits, and the inductive limit $\varinjlim_{J(X)}$ commutes with finite projective limits
because $J(X)$ is a cofiltered set (I 2.7).

2) Let $X$ be an object of $C$, and let $f,g : X \to LF$ be two morphisms that coincide on a refinement $R \to X$ of
$X$. By virtue of 3.1 2), there exists a covering sieve $R' \to X$, which one may always suppose contained in $R$, and
two morphisms $u,v : R' \to F$ such that $Z_{R'}(u)=f$ and $Z_{R'}(v)=g$. By virtue of 3.1 1), we then have
$\ell(F)\circ u=\ell(F)\circ v$. Consequently (3.1 4)) $u$ and $v$ coincide on a refinement $R'' \to R'$. Let $w$ be
the restriction of $u$ to $R''$. We have $Z_{R''}(w)=Z_{R'}(u)=Z_{R'}(v)$, and consequently $f=g$. The presheaf $LF$ is
therefore separated.

3) If $F$ is separated, the morphism $\ell(F)$ is a monomorphism because a filtered inductive limit of monomorphisms is
a monomorphism. If $\ell(F)$ is a monomorphism, the presheaf $F$ is a subpresheaf of a separated presheaf, hence is
separated. We show that $LF$ is then a sheaf. Let $i : R \to X$ be a sieve covering an object $X$ of $C$, and let
$u : R \to LF$ be a morphism. It suffices to show that $u$ factors through $X$. Put $R'=F \times_{LF} R$ and
$v=Z_{R'}(\operatorname{pr}_1)$:

```text
F  --ell(F)--> LF
^              ^ \ v
| pr_1         | u
R' --pr_2---> R --i--> X
^              ^
| p_1          | m
R'' --p_2--->  Y
```

[PDF p. 249]

To show that $u=v\circ i$, it suffices to show (I 3.4) that for every morphism $m : Y \to R$ ($Y$ an object of $C$),
$v\circ i\circ m=u\circ m$. Put $R''=Y \times_R R'$ and let $p_1$ and $p_2$ be the projections. The projection $R'' \to
Y$ is a monomorphism and makes $R''$ into a sieve covering $Y$ (3.1 2)). We have $v\circ i\circ

## 4. Exactness Properties of the Category of Sheaves

The exactness properties of the category of sheaves are deduced from the exactness properties of the category of
presheaves via Theorem 3.4. The present section makes this philosophy explicit in typical statements among the most
useful ones.

**Theorem 4.1.** Let $C$ be a $U$-site, $\widetilde{C}$ the category of sheaves, $a : \widehat{C} \to \widetilde{C}$ the
associated sheaf functor, and $i : \widetilde{C} \to \widehat{C}$ the inclusion functor.

1. The functor $a$ commutes with inductive limits and is exact.

[PDF p. 252]

2. $U$-inductive limits in $\widetilde{C}$ are representable. For every small category $I$ and every functor $E : I \to
   \widetilde{C}$, the canonical morphism

   $$
   \varinjlim_I E \to a(\varinjlim_I iE)
   $$

   is an isomorphism.

3. $U$-projective limits in $\widetilde{C}$ are representable. For every object $X$ of $C$, the functor on
   $\widetilde{C}$

   $$
   F \mapsto F(X)
   $$

   commutes with projective limits, i.e. the inclusion functor $i : \widetilde{C} \to \widehat{C}$ commutes with
   projective limits.

**Proof.** These properties result essentially from Theorem 3.4 and from (I 2.11).

Thus, in the category of sheaves, products indexed by an element of $U$, fiber products, sums indexed by an element of
$U$, amalgamated sums, kernels, cokernels, images, and coimages are representable.

**Corollary 4.1.1.** Let $C$ be a $U$-site and $F$ a sheaf of sets on $C$. The canonical homomorphism

$$ \varinjlim_{C/F} aX \to F $$

is an isomorphism.

**Proof.** This follows from (I 3.4) and from the fact that $a$ commutes with inductive limits.

**Proposition 4.2.** Every morphism of $\widetilde{C}$ that is both an epimorphism and a monomorphism is an isomorphism.

**Proof.** Let $u : G \to H$ be a morphism of $\widetilde{C}$ that is an epimorphism and a monomorphism. First note that
the morphism $u$ is a monomorphism

[PDF p. 253]

of presheaves (4.1 1)). Construct the amalgamated sum $H \amalg_G H=K$ and the two canonical morphisms $i_1,i_2 : H \to
K$ in the category of presheaves. Since $u$ is a monomorphism of presheaves, one verifies immediately that the following
diagram is cartesian and cocartesian (I 10.1):

```text
G --u--> H
|        |
u        i_2
v        v
H --i_1-> K
```

Applying the "associated sheaf" functor, we therefore obtain a cartesian and cocartesian diagram of the category of
sheaves (4.1 1)):

```text
G --u-->  H
|         |
u         a i_2
v         v
H --a i_1-> aK
```

Since $u$ is an epimorphism of sheaves, the morphism $a i_1$ is an isomorphism; and since the diagram (*) is cartesian,
the morphism $u$ is an isomorphism.

**Proposition 4.3.**

1. Inductive limits in $\widetilde{C}$ that are representable are universal (I 2.5).
2. Every epimorphic family (I 10.2) of morphisms is universally effective epimorphic (2.6).
3. Every equivalence relation is universally effective (I 10.6).
4. Filtered $U$-inductive limits commute with finite projective limits (I 2.6).

[PDF p. 254]

**Proof.** Assertions 1) to 4) are true in the category of sets, hence in the category of presheaves (I 3.1). Assertions
1) and 4) then follow immediately from the corresponding assertions for presheaves and from 4.1. Let us prove 3). Let
$p_1,p_2 : R \to X$ be an equivalence relation of $\widetilde{C}$. The diagram

$$ R \mathrel{\substack{\xrightarrow{p_1}\\[-0.4ex]\xrightarrow[p_2]{}}} X $$

is then an equivalence relation of presheaves (4.1 1)). There therefore exists a morphism of presheaves $u : X \to Y$
such that the diagram

```text

|          |
p_1        u
v          v

```

is cartesian and cocartesian in the category of presheaves. Applying the "associated sheaf" functor, one obtains a
cartesian and cocartesian diagram in the category of sheaves (4.1 1)). Consequently $R \to X \times X$ is an effective
equivalence relation. Since every equivalence relation is effective by what precedes, every equivalence relation is
universally effective.

Let us prove 2). Let $(u_i : X_i \to X)_{i \in I}$ be an epimorphic family of $\widetilde{C}$. By (II 2.6) and (I 2.12),
it suffices to prove the following assertions:

a) For every morphism of sheaves $v : Y \to X$, the family of morphisms $\operatorname{pr}_{2,i} : X_i \times_X Y \to
Y$, $i \in I$, is an epimorphic family of $\widetilde{C}$.

b) For every sheaf $Z$, the diagram of sets

$$ \operatorname{Hom}(X,Z) \to \prod_{i\in I}\operatorname{Hom}(X_i,Z) \rightrightarrows \prod_{(i,k)\in I\times
I}\operatorname{Hom}(X_i\times_X X_k,Z) $$

is exact.

[PDF p. 255]

and the morphism $j$, and the family $u'_i : X_i \to X'$, $i \in I$, is an epimorphic family of $\widehat{C}$. Since $j$
is a monomorphism, the morphism $a j : aX' \to X$ is a monomorphism of sheaves (4.1). Since, for every $i \in I$, we

$$ \operatorname{Hom}(X',Z) \to \prod_{i\in I}\operatorname{Hom}(X_i,Z) \rightrightarrows \prod_{(i,k)\in I\times
I}\operatorname{Hom}(X_i\times_{X'}X_k,Z) $$

is exact. Finally, since $X'$ is a subpresheaf of $X$, the presheaf $X_i \times_{X'} X_k$, $(i,k)\in I\times I$, is
canonically isomorphic to $X_i \times_X X_k$, which proves b).

**Definition 4.5.** An initial object of a category $A$ is an object $\varnothing_A$ representing the empty inductive
limit, i.e. such that, for every $X \in \operatorname{ob} A$, there exists one and only one arrow $\varnothing_A \to X$.
An object $\varnothing_A$ is said to be **strictly initial** if it is initial and if every morphism with target
$\varnothing_A$ is an isomorphism. Let $(S_i)_{i \in I}$ be a family of objects of a category $A$. Suppose that the sum
$S=\coprod_{i\in I}S_i$ is representable. The sum $S$ is said to be **disjoint** if the structural morphisms $S_i \to S$
are squarable, if they are monomorphisms, and if, for every pair $i,j \in I$, $i\neq j$, the product $S_i \times_S S_j$
is an initial object of $A$. The sum $S$ is said to be disjoint

[PDF p. 260]

**universal** if it is disjoint and if it remains a disjoint sum after every base change $T \to S$. It follows that for
every pair $i,j$, $i\neq j$, the objects $S_i \times_S S_j$ are strictly initial objects of $A$.

**Example 4.5.1.** In the category of sets, direct sums are disjoint and universal. The same is therefore true in every
category of presheaves of sets (I 3.1), and consequently in every category of sheaves of sets on a $U$-site (4.1); in
particular, the initial object of $\widetilde{C}$ is strict.

**Proposition 4.6.** Let $C$ be a $U$-category and let $(s_i : X_i \to X)_{i \in I}$ be a family of morphisms of $C$.
For every $U$-topology $\mathcal{G}$ on $C$ (3.0.2), denote by $\widetilde{C}_{\mathcal{G}}$ the corresponding category
of sheaves and by $\varepsilon_{\mathcal{G}} : C \to \widetilde{C}_{\mathcal{G}}$ the corresponding functor.

1. Let $\mathcal{G}$ be a $U$-topology such that

   $$

$$ \Delta_i : X_i \to X_i \times_X X_i $$

is transformed by the "associated sheaf" functor (for $\mathcal{G}$) into an isomorphism, which is the case if the $s_i$
are monomorphisms.

c) For every pair $(i,j)$, $i\neq j$, of elements of $I$, the presheaf $X_i \times_X X_j$ is transformed by the

ii) $X$ is a strictly initial object of $C$.

**Proof.** Take the empty set as the set $I$ in 4.6.3.

**Proof.** ii) $\Rightarrow$ i). The canonical morphism $R \to X \times_Y X$ is an isomorphism. Let $S \to Y$ be the

## 5. Extension of a Topology from $C$ to $\widehat{C}$

**Proposition 5.1.** Let $C$ be a $U$-site and let $f : H \to K$ be a morphism of $\widehat{C}$. The following
conditions are equivalent:

i) For every $X \to K$, with $X \in \operatorname{ob} C$, the corresponding morphism $H \times_K X \to X$ has as image a
covering sieve of $X$.

ii) The morphism $a(f) : aH \to aK$ on associated sheaves is an

[PDF p. 268]

epimorphism of $\widetilde{C}$.

ii bis) For every sheaf $F$ on $C$, the map $\operatorname{Hom}(K,F)\to \operatorname{Hom}(H,F)$ deduced from $f$ is
injective.

**Proof.** It is clear that ii) is equivalent to ii bis).

Let us prove that ii) $\Rightarrow$ i). By virtue of 4.4, i) means that $a(H \times_K X)\to a(X)$ is an epimorphism.
But, since $a(H \times_K X)\simeq a(H)\times_{a(K)}a(X)$, the functor $a$ commuting with finite limits (4.1), the
morphism under consideration is obtained from $a(f) : a(H)\to a(K)$ by base change. Since epimorphisms of
$\widetilde{C}$ are universal, this proves the implication ii) $\Rightarrow$ i). Conversely, since the family of the
$X_i \to K$ is epimorphic, the same is true of the family of the $a(X_i)\to a(K)$ in $\widetilde{C}$ (4.1). Thus, to
verify that $a(f) : a(H)\to a(K)$ is epimorphic, it suffices to see it after every base change of the preceding type
$a(X)\to a(K)$, which proves i) $\Rightarrow$ ii), QED.

**Definition 5.2.**

1. A morphism $f : H \to K$ satisfying the three equivalent conditions of 5.1 is called a **covering morphism**. A
   family of morphisms $f_i : H_i \to K$, $i\in I$, with the same target is said to be **covering** if the corresponding
   morphism $f : \coprod_i H_i \to K$ is covering.

2. A morphism $f : H \to K$ is said to be **bicovering** if it is covering and if the diagonal morphism $H \to H\times_K
   H$ is covering. A family $f_i : H_i \to K$, $i\in I$, with the same target is said to be **bicovering** if the
   corresponding morphism $f : \coprod_i H_i \to K$ is bicovering.

By condition i) of 5.1, to say that a family $(f_i : H_i \to K)$, $i\in I$, is covering means that, for every morphism
$X \to K$, with $X \in \operatorname{ob} C$, the family of $H_i \times_K X \to X$, $i\in I$, has as image a sieve
covering

[PDF p. 269]

$X$; or again, by ii), that the family of morphisms $a(f_i) : aH_i \to aK$ in $\widetilde{C}$ is epimorphic, taking into
account that the functor $a$ commutes with direct sums (4.1). (*)

**Proposition 5.3.** Let $C$ be a $U$-site and let $f : H \to K$ be a morphism of $\widehat{C}$. The following
conditions are equivalent:

i) The morphism $f$ is bicovering (5.2 2)).

i bis) The morphism $f$ is covering and, for every object $X$ of $C$ and every pair of morphisms $u,v : X \to H$ such
that $fu=fv$, the kernel of $(u,v)$ is a sieve covering $X$.

ii) The morphism $a(f) : aH \to aK$ is an isomorphism of $\widetilde{C}$.

ii bis) For every sheaf $F$ on $C$, the map $\operatorname{Hom}(K,F)\to \operatorname{Hom}(H,F)$ is a bijection.

**Proof.** The equivalence i) $\Longleftrightarrow$ i bis) follows from condition i) of 5.1, applied to the diagonal
morphism $H \to H\times_K H$; the equivalence ii) $\Longleftrightarrow$ ii bis) is trivial.

i) $\Rightarrow$ ii). The morphism $a(f) : aH \to aK$ is an epimorphism (5.1). Since the functor $a$ commutes with the
formation of fiber products (4.1), the diagonal morphism $aH \to aH\times_{aK}aH$ is an epimorphism (5.1). Since the
diagonal morphism is always a monomorphism, it is an isomorphism (4.2). Consequently the morphism $a(f)$ is a
monomorphism. It is therefore an isomorphism (4.2).

ii) $\Rightarrow$ i). Since the functor $a$ commutes with the formation of fiber products, the morphism $f$ and the
diagonal morphism $H \to H\times_K H$ are transformed by $a$ into isomorphisms. In particular, they are transformed by
$a$ into epimorphisms. They are therefore covering, QED.

(*) Note that the property, for a morphism or a family of morphisms of $\widehat{C}$, of being covering (respectively
bicovering) is stable under base change.

[PDF p. 270]

**5.3.1.** It follows from 5.3, and from the fact that $a$ commutes with direct sums, that a family $(f_i : H_i \to
K)_{i\in I}$ of morphisms of $\widehat{C}$ is bicovering if and only if the $f_i$ induce on associated sheaves an
isomorphism from the direct sum $\coprod_i aH_i$ onto $aK$; or again if and only if, for every sheaf $F$, the map

$$ \operatorname{Hom}(K,F)\to \prod_i \operatorname{Hom}(H_i,F) $$

is bijective.

**Proposition 5.4.** Let $C$ be a $U$-site. There exists on $\widehat{C}$ a topology, evidently unique, such that a
family $(H_i \to K)_{i\in I}$ of arrows of $\widehat{C}$ with the same target is covering for this topology if and only
if it is covering in the sense of 5.2. This is also the least fine topology on $\widehat{C}$ among the topologies $T$
having the following properties:

- a) $T$ is finer than the canonical topology of $\widehat{C}$, that is, every epimorphic family of $\widehat{C}$ is
  covering for $T$.
- b) Every covering family in $C$ is covering in $\widehat{C}$.

**Proof.** We shall confine ourselves to giving indications. We leave to the reader the task of showing that the
covering families in the sense of 5.2 are the covering families of a topology $T_C$ on $\widehat{C}$; one uses 4.1. The
covering families of the canonical topology on $\widehat{C}$ are the epimorphic families on $\widehat{C}$ (2.6 and I
3.1). Since $a$ commutes with inductive limits (4.1), the topology $T_C$ is finer than the canonical topology of
$\widehat{C}$.

Moreover the covering families of $C$ are covering families of $T_C$ (5.1). Thus if $T'$ denotes the least fine of the
topologies on $\widehat{C}$ possessing properties a) and b), $T_C$ is finer than $T'$. Let $(f_i : H_i \to K)_{i\in I}$
be a covering family of $T_C$. We show that $(f_i)_{i\in I}$ is a covering family of $T'$.

[PDF p. 271]

Let $s_i : H_i \to H=\coprod_i H_i$ be the canonical monomorphisms, and let $f=(f_i)_{i\in I} : H=\coprod_i H_i \to K$
be the morphism defined by the $f_i$. The family of the $s_i$ is covering for $T'$. To show that $(f_i)_{i\in I}$ is a
covering family of $T'$, it therefore suffices, by virtue of axiom (T2) for topologies, to show that the morphism $f : H
\to K$ is covering for $T'$.

There exists an epimorphic family of $\widehat{C}$, $u_{\lambda} : X_{\lambda}\to K$, $\lambda\in \Lambda$, with
$X_{\lambda}\in \operatorname{ob}C$ (I 3.4). To show that $f : H \to K$ is covering for $T'$, it therefore suffices,
again by axiom (T2), to show that, for every $\lambda\in \Lambda$, the morphism

$$ \operatorname{pr}_2 : H\times_K X_{\lambda}\to X_{\lambda} $$

is covering for $T'$. Let $v_j : Y_j \to H\times_K X_{\lambda}$, $j\in J$, $Y_j\in \operatorname{ob}C$, be an epimorphic
family of $\widehat{C}$. The family $(\operatorname{pr}_2 v_j)_{j\in J}$ is covering for $T_C$. It is therefore a
covering family of $C$ (5.1), and then a covering family of $T'$. Consequently the sieve generated by
$\operatorname{pr}_2 : H\times_K X_{\lambda}\to X_{\lambda}$ contains a sieve covering for $T'$. It is therefore
covering for $T'$, and consequently $\operatorname{pr}_2 : H\times_K X_{\lambda}\to X_{\lambda}$ is covering, QED.

**Remark 5.4.1.** The proof of 5.4 shows in fact that every topology $T'$ on $\widehat{C}$, finer than the canonical
topology of $\widehat{C}$, is the least fine of the topologies $T$ on $\widehat{C}$ that possess the following
properties:

- a) $T$ is finer than the canonical topology of $\widehat{C}$.
- b) Every covering family for $T'$ of the form $(u_i : X_i \to X)_{i\in I}$, where $X$ and the $X_i$ are objects of
  $C$, is covering for $T$.

In particular, every topology $T'$ on $\widehat{C}$, finer than the canonical topology, is uniquely determined by the
families $(u_i : X_i \to X)_{i\in I}$, with $X$ and the $X_i$ objects of $C$, that are covering for $T'$.

[PDF p. 272]

**Remark 5.4.2.** One can easily show that, for every topology $T'$ on $\widehat{C}$ finer than the canonical topology,
the set of families of morphisms $(X_i \to X)_{i\in I}$ with the same target in $C$ that are covering for $T'$ is the
set of covering families of a topology on $C$. Thus 5.4 and 5.4.1 make it possible to establish a one-to-one
correspondence between topologies on $C$ and topologies on $\widehat{C}$ finer than the canonical topology.

**5.5.0.** Let $C$ be a small category. Denote by $\operatorname{Caf}$ the set of strictly full subcategories of
$\widehat{C}$ (that is, such that every object isomorphic to an object of the subcategory is again an object of the
subcategory) whose injection functor admits a left adjoint that commutes with finite projective limits. Also denote by
$\mathcal{T}$ the set of topologies on $C$.

Theorem 3.4 defines a map

$$ \Phi : \mathcal{T} \to \operatorname{Caf}. $$

We shall define a map in the opposite direction. For this, one must associate to every element

$$ e=(i' : C' \rightleftarrows \widehat{C},\ a'\text{ left adjoint to }i') $$

a topology $T_e$ on $C$. For every object $X$ of $C$, we define $J_e(X)$ to be the set of subobjects of $X$ whose
injection morphism is transformed by $a'$ into an isomorphism. Using the hypotheses made on $a'$, one verifies
immediately that this defines a topology $T_e$ on $C$. We have therefore defined a map

$$ \Psi : \operatorname{Caf} \to \mathcal{T}. $$

We then have the following result, due to J. Giraud:

[PDF p. 273]

**Theorem 5.5.** The map $\Phi$ is a bijection, and $\Psi$ is its inverse.

**Proof.** The map $\Psi\circ \Phi$ is the identity. Indeed, this follows immediately from 5.1.

The map $\Phi\circ \Psi$ is the identity. Indeed, let $(i' : C' \rightleftarrows \widehat{C}, a'\text{ left adjoint to
}i')$ be an element of $\operatorname{Caf}$, let $T_e$ be the topology corresponding to it by $\Psi$, and let $C_e$ be
the category of sheaves for $T_e$. Using the definition of the topology $T_e$ and the definition of bicovering morphisms
(5.2), one then proves the equivalence of the following assertions:

- i) The morphism $u$ of $\widehat{C}$ is bicovering for the topology $T_e$.
- ii) The morphism $u$ of $\widehat{C}$ is transformed by $a'$ into an isomorphism.

It is clear that $C'$ is a full subcategory of $C_e$. It therefore suffices to show that every sheaf $F$ for the
topology $T_e$ is an object of $C'$. But, by the equivalence above, the morphism

$$ F \to i'\circ a'(F) $$

is bicovering; since its source and target are sheaves, 5.3 (ii) implies that $F \to i'\circ a'(F)$ is an isomorphism,
QED.

## 6. Sheaves with Values in a Category

**6.0.** Let $C$ and $D$ be two categories. A contravariant functor from $C$ to $D$,

$$ F : C^{\circ} \to D, $$

is called a presheaf on $C$ with values in $D$.

**Definition 6.1.** Let $C$ be a site and $D$ a category. A presheaf $F : C^{\circ} \to D$ is called a sheaf on $C$ with
values in $D$, or more briefly a sheaf with values in $D$, if for every object $S$ of $D$, the presheaf of sets

$$ X \mapsto \operatorname{Hom}_D(S,F(X)),\qquad X\in \operatorname{ob}(C), $$

is a sheaf. The full subcategory of $\operatorname{Hom}(C^{\circ},D)$ formed by sheaves on $C$ with values in $D$ will
be denoted $\widetilde{\operatorname{Hom}}(C^{\circ},D)$.

[PDF p. 274]

**Remarks 6.2.**

1. Condition 6.1 means that, for every object $X$ of $C$, every covering sieve $R$ of $X$, and every object $S$ of $D$,
   one has an isomorphism (I 3.5 and 2.1)

   $$
   \operatorname{Hom}_D(S,F(X)) \simeq
   \varprojlim_{C/R}\operatorname{Hom}_D(S,F(-)).
   $$

   Thus, when $D$ is the category of $U$-sets, one recovers the definition of sheaves of sets (loc. cit.).

2. Let $D'$ be a $U$-category and let $G : D \to D'$ be a functor commuting with projective limits. The functor $G$
   transforms, by composition, sheaves with values in $D$ into sheaves with values in $D'$.

**6.3.0.** Let $\gamma$ be a species of algebraic structure defined by finite projective limits (I 2.9), and let
$U$-$\gamma$ be the category of $\gamma$-sets belonging to $U$. Denote by $\operatorname{ens} : U\text{-}\gamma \to
U\text{-Ens}$ the underlying set functor. The functor $\operatorname{ens}$ commutes with projective limits and,
consequently, by the preceding remark, defines by composition the functors

$$ \operatorname{ens}^{\wedge} : \operatorname{Hom}(C^{\circ},U\text{-}\gamma) \to \widehat{C} $$

$$ \widetilde{\operatorname{ens}} : \widetilde{\operatorname{Hom}}(C^{\circ},U\text{-}\gamma) \to \widetilde{C}. $$

The functor $\widetilde{\operatorname{ens}}$ is called the "underlying sheaf of sets" functor. In fact, it factors
canonically through the category $\gamma$-$\widetilde{C}$ of $\gamma$-objects of $\widetilde{C}$, and, denoting the
resulting functor again by $\widetilde{\operatorname{ens}}$,

$$ \widetilde{\operatorname{Hom}}(C^{\circ},U\text{-}\gamma) \to \gamma\text{-}\widetilde{C}, $$

one can state:

**Proposition 6.3.1.** The functor $\widetilde{\operatorname{ens}}$ establishes an equivalence of categories between the
category of sheaves on $C$ with values in $U$-$\gamma$, the category of $\gamma$-objects of the category of sheaves of
sets, and the category of $\gamma$-objects of the category of presheaves of sets whose underlying presheaf is a sheaf.

[PDF p. 275]

**Proof.** The proof essentially uses (I 3.2) and the fact that a finite projective limit of sheaves in the category of
presheaves is a sheaf (4.1).

**6.3.2.** The preceding proposition justifies the abuse of language that consists in identifying a sheaf with values in

We shall use the classical terminology: sheaves of groups, sheaves of commutative groups, which we shall most often call

**6.3.3.** We denote by $\widetilde{C}_{\gamma}$, respectively $\widehat{C}_{\gamma}$, the category of $\gamma$-objects

**Proposition 6.4.** The inclusion functor $i_{\gamma} : \widetilde{C}_{\gamma}\to \widehat{C}_{\gamma}$ admits a left

$$ (*)\quad i_A(\operatorname{Ker}(u)) \simeq \operatorname{Ker}(i_A(u)), $$

$$ (***)\quad \operatorname{coim}(u) \simeq a_A\operatorname{coim}(i_A(u)). $$

is left exact and commutes with arbitrary inductive limits. Consequently it is additive, exact, and commutes with

is denoted $A_H$. When $H=a(X)$, the sheaf associated to the presheaf represented by $X$, one sometimes simply writes
$A_X$, by abuse of notation. The proof of 6.7 shows that the family of sheaves of $A$-modules $A_X$, $X\in
\operatorname{ob}(C)$, is a generating family, indexed by an element of $U$, of the category of sheaves of $A$-modules.

**Remark 6.9.** By [TOHOKU], 6.7 shows that the category $\widetilde{C}_A$, when $A$ is an element of $U$, has enough
injectives. It is known, moreover, that infinite products are not necessarily exact in $\widetilde{C}_A$, and
consequently that the category $\widetilde{C}_A$ does not in general have enough projectives [Roos].
