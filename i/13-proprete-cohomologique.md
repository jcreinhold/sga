# Expos√© XIII. Cohomological Properness of Sheaves of Sets and of Sheaves of Noncommutative Groups

<!-- label: XIII -->

<!-- original page 344 -->

By Mme M. Raynaud. [Translator note: according to unpublished notes of A. Grothendieck.]

This expos√© proposes to use √©tale cohomology to generalize certain results of Expos√©s IX and X. It also shows how one
can extend to sheaves of not necessarily commutative groups those results of SGA 5 II that still make sense for such
sheaves. The notions of √©tale cohomology set out in SGA 4 are assumed known.

The main result (XIII.2.4) gives an important example of a nonproper morphism `f: U ‚Üí S` that is "cohomologically proper
in dimension ‚Č§ 1," that is, such that, for certain sheaves of groups `F` on `U`, in the sense of the √©tale topology, the
formation of `f_*F` and `R¬Ļf_*F` commutes with every base change `S‚Ä≤ ‚Üí S`. This property is indeed satisfied by the open
`U` of a scheme `X` proper over `S`, the complement of a divisor `D` with normal crossings relative to `S`, at least if
one requires `F` to be finite constant of order prime to the residual characteristics of `S`. If `F` is no longer
assumed of order prime to the residual characteristics of `S`, one has an analogous result by replacing `R¬Ļf_*F` by the
subsheaf `R¬Ļ_tame f_*F` obtained by restricting to torsors under `F` "tamely ramified on `X` relative to `S`." In
particular, this makes it possible to show that the tamely ramified fundamental group of a proper smooth algebraic curve
over a separably closed field, with finitely many closed points removed, is topologically of finite type (XIII.2.12).

## 0. Reminders on the Theory of Stacks

<!-- label: XIII.0 -->

We shall use in what follows the theory of stacks set out in [XIII.1] and [XIII.2]. We restrict ourselves to the case of
the √©tale site of a scheme. Given a scheme X, write X_et for the √©tale site of X. Recall that a stack F on X is a
fibered category over X_et such that, for every scheme X‚Ä≤ √©tale over X and every pair of objects x, y of the fiber F_X‚Ä≤,
the presheaf SheafHom_X‚Ä≤(x,y) is a sheaf, and such that, for every surjective √©tale morphism X‚Ä≥ ‚Üí X‚Ä≤, every object of
F_X‚Ä≥ endowed with a descent datum relative to X‚Ä≥ ‚Üí X‚Ä≤ is the inverse image of an object of F_X‚Ä≤. \[Translator note: the
corrected source writes ‚Äústack F‚ÄĚ and corrects ‚Äúpair of object‚ÄĚ to ‚Äúpair of objects.‚ÄĚ\]

We write F(X‚Ä≤) for the category of cartesian sections of F/X‚Ä≤. More generally, if Sch_X is the category of schemes over
X endowed with the √©tale topology, the stack F may be extended to a stack ūĚďē on Sch_X, and for every morphism f: X‚Ä≤ ‚Üí X,
one again writes F(X‚Ä≤) for the category of cartesian sections of this stack ūĚďē over X‚Ä≤.

A gerbe is a stack such that, for every scheme X‚Ä≤ √©tale over X and every pair of objects x, y of F_X‚Ä≤, every morphism
from x to y is an isomorphism, x and y are locally isomorphic, and the set of objects X‚Ä≤ of X_et such that F_X‚Ä≤ is
nonempty is a refinement of X_et. For example, the stack of torsors under a sheaf of groups is a gerbe which, moreover,
has a cartesian section. Conversely, a gerbe which has a section, that is, such that there exists an object x of F_X, is
equivalent to the stack of torsors under the sheaf

<!-- original page 346 -->

of groups SheafAut_X(x).

There is an evident notion of subgerbe and maximal subgerbe of a stack F. Given a cartesian section x of F(X), there
exists a unique maximal subgerbe G_x of F such that x factors through G_x. One calls G_x the subgerbe generated by x; it
is by definition a trivial gerbe. The presheaf SF

<!-- label: indnot:mb -->

defined by

```text
SF(X‚Ä≤) = { maximal subgerbes of F|X‚Ä≤ }
```

is a sheaf, called the sheaf of maximal subgerbes of F. Let O be the presheaf defined by

```text
O(X‚Ä≤) = { classes of objects of F_X‚Ä≤ modulo isomorphism }.
```

By associating to every object x of F_X‚Ä≤ the maximal subgerbe of F|X‚Ä≤ generated by x, one obtains a morphism

```text

```

by [XIII.2, III 2.1.4], this morphism makes SF a sheaf associated with O.

A stack F is said to be **constructible**, respectively **ind-‚Ąí-finite**, where ‚Ąí is a set of prime numbers, if for
every scheme X‚Ä≤ √©tale over X and every object x of F_X‚Ä≤, the same is true of the sheaf SheafAut_X‚Ä≤(x) \[XIII.2, VII
2.2.1\]. A stack is said to be **1-constructible** if it is constructible and if its sheaf of maximal subgerbes is
constructible.

## 2. A Special Case of Cohomological Properness: Relative Normal-Crossings Divisors

<!-- label: XIII.2 -->

### 2.0.

<!-- label: XIII.2.0 -->

Let `R` be a discrete valuation ring with field of fractions `K`, and let `L` be an √©tale `K`-algebra. Then `L` is a
direct product of finitely many fields `L_i`, where `L_i` is an √©tale extension of `K`. If `L‚Ä≤_i` denotes the Galois
extension generated by `L_i` in an algebraic closure of `L_i`, one says that `L` is **tamely ramified** over `R` if the
`L‚Ä≤_i` are tamely ramified extensions in the sense of X.3, that is, if an inertia group `I_i` of `L‚Ä≤_i|K` has order
prime to the residual characteristic `p` of `R`.

One knows that `I_i` is in any case an extension of a cyclic group of order prime to `p` by a `p`-group. This follows
from [XIII.5, ch. IV, prop. 7, cor. 4] when the residual extension of `R` is separable. The proof given there extends to
the general case as follows. Resume the hypotheses and notation of loc. cit., but without assuming the residual
extension separable. Let `H_i` be the subgroup of the inertia group `G‚āÄ` consisting of the elements `s` of `G‚āÄ` such
that `sŌÄ/ŌÄ ‚ąą U^i` for every uniformizer `ŌÄ` of `A_L`. One then verifies that `G‚āÄ/H‚āĀ` is a group of order prime to `p`
and that, for `i ‚Č• 1`, the `H^i/H^{i+1}` are `p`-groups, from which the announced result follows.

### 2.0.1.

<!-- label: XIII.2.0.1 -->

In the case where `R` is strictly local, one has the following simple characterization: the `K`-algebra `L` is tamely
ramified over `R` if and only if the `[L_i:K]` are prime to `p`. Moreover, if `L` is tamely ramified over `R`, the `L_i`
are cyclic extensions of `K`. Indeed, when `R` is strictly local, `I_i` is equal to the Galois group

<!-- original page 370 -->

of `L‚Ä≤_i` over `K`. As just recalled, `I_i` is an extension of a cyclic group of order prime to `p` by a `p`-group. If
`L‚Ä≤_i` is assumed tamely ramified over `R`, `I_i` is then a cyclic group of order prime to `p`. It follows that
`[L_i:K]` is prime to `p` and that `L_i = L‚Ä≤_i`. Conversely, if `[L_i:K]` is prime to `p`, `I_i` cannot contain a
nontrivial normal `p`-subgroup; hence `I_i` is a cyclic group of order prime to `p`, which proves that `L_i` is tamely
ramified over `R`.

**Remark (M. Raynaud, added in 2003).**

Let k be an algebraically closed field of characteristic p > 0, let X be a connected proper smooth algebraic curve over
